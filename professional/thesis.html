Master Thesis  ·  Harvard Medical School & ETH Zurich  ·  Sep 2025 – Mar 2026

Automated Carotid Artery Analysis Pipeline

A fully automated, modular framework for multi-domain characterization of carotid stenosis from CT angiography — integrating deep learning segmentation, morphological analysis, calcification mapping, and 3D CFD simulation.

GitHub → Harvard Surgery Research Day →

Background

Carotid stenosis — the narrowing of the arteries that supply blood to the brain — affects approximately 4% of the general population and is identified in up to 20% of stroke patients. With age, prevalence rises to around 7.5% in men and 5% in women over 80.

Despite the complexity of the disease, current clinical assessment relies on a single linear diameter measurement and operator-dependent blood velocity readings. Evidence increasingly shows that disease outcomes depend on plaque morphology, calcification distribution, and local hemodynamics — none of which are captured by standard techniques. The result is high variability in treatment decisions for patients with identical stenosis severity grades.

A comprehensive, automated, and reproducible characterization framework was missing. This thesis addresses that gap.

The Pipeline

A fully automated bash/Python framework that takes raw CTA scans as input and produces a structured, multi-modal feature dataset across three domains: vessel morphology, calcification, and hemodynamics. Each stage is implemented as an independent, configurable module — enabling users to swap components without disrupting the overall workflow.

01
Input Raw CTA scans (111 patients) in DICOM format. Processed on standard research computing hardware with optional GPU acceleration.
02
Deep Learning Segmentation Five configurable models — nnU-Net, SegMamba, UNETR, SwinUNETR, UMambaBot — benchmarked on an iteratively expanded annotation set. nnU-Net trained with progressive data augmentation; qualitative and quantitative comparison across challenging segmentation cases.
03
ROI Isolation & Standardization Automated carotid bifurcation extraction, centerline computation, and geometric standardization — ensuring consistent spatial orientation across all patients for meaningful cohort-level comparisons.
04
Morphological Feature Extraction Centerline-based descriptors: diameter profiles, cross-sectional area, tortuosity, curvature, torsion, bifurcation and planarity angles, and stenosis severity (NASCET/ECST). Computed along the ICA, ECA, and CCA branches.
05
Calcification Characterization Calcium segmentation via Hounsfield unit thresholding. Spatial distribution quantified longitudinally, circumferentially, and radially along the vessel wall — capturing plaque burden beyond a single volume estimate.
06
3D CFD Haemodynamic Simulation Two configurable solvers — SimVascular and ANSYS Fluent (via PyFluent) — with three outlet boundary conditions: traction-free, Murray's Law, and Windkessel. Extracts wall shear stress (WSS), pressure, and velocity fields across the cardiac cycle. Mesh generated automatically from patient geometry.
07
Output Dataset 354 features per patient (177 per carotid) across morphological, calcification, and haemodynamic domains. Structured for downstream ML-based prediction of post-surgical stroke risk. The Nezami Lab is currently building a predictive model on top of this dataset.

Automated segmentations closely matched clinician annotations — validated via Bland-Altman analysis across all morphological features, with high Pearson correlation and low relative differences. CFD haemodynamic outputs showed good-to-excellent agreement against an experimental reference workflow.

Most pipeline failures were attributable to upstream segmentation quality: CTA image artifacts or segmentation errors caused cascading failures in downstream modules. Improvements at this stage would substantially raise the end-to-end success rate.

The pipeline eliminates operator-dependent variability across all stages, enabling reproducible large-cohort characterization with consistent feature definitions — a prerequisite for meaningful between-patient comparisons and outcome modeling.
